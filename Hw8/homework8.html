For this assignment, I was curious to see the percentages of people in Pennsylvania over the age of 25 for which a high school degree was their highest degree recieved. The ratio that I made was [(total number of people in a county whose highest degree was a high school diploma / total population for that county) * 100]. This is different from the data I used in homework 7 (that data had counties missing and also did not translate well to a ratio), so a simple population map is at the bottom of this page for reference.


Equal Interval

Equal Interval is a method of classification in which the range of each category is the same (in this map, the range of each is 7%). This is best for data that is very evenly distributed, with no large clumps of data in any one interval. This map is potentially the most straightforward to understand when first looking at it, but does not provide as much detail as it could (for example, a majority of counties on this map fall into the 42-49% category).


Equal Count (Quantile)

Equal Count is a method of classification in which each category has a similar number of data points (in this case, counties). This is similar to Equal Interval, but accounts for possible clumping of data by evening out the size of the categories. For example, the category represented by the second-darkest shade of purple that was so prevalent in the previous map has now been redistributed. Trends are easier to see at first glance because less of the map is the same color. However, since the categories do not all have the same range, this map is harder to fully understand without reading the key.


Natural Breaks

Natural Breaks is a method of classification in which clusters of data are taken into account. The data will be sorted into categories based on patterns in the data, not based on the range or size of the invervals themselves. Because of this, the map is fairly accurate when you are glancing at it to see trends, but this mode could also result in categories with either very large or very small ranges, depending on the data being used.
